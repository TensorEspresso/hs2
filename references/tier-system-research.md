# HS2 Tier System Research

Research into how US health systems structure their facilities hierarchically, with focus on whether clinics belong in Tier 2 or Tier 3.

**Date:** June 26, 2026
**Conclusion:** Clinics should be Tier 3. Evidence consistently shows a 3-level structure below the health system root.

---

## Recommended Tier Structure

| Tier | Entity Type | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Root (0) | Health System | Umbrella organization |
| Tier 1 | Hospitals / Medical Centers | Inpatient, full-service, resource-intensive anchors |
| Tier 2 | Specialty Centers / Ambulatory Care Centers | Focused service array, branded entities with independent identity |
| Tier 3 | Clinics / Satellite Locations / Provider Practices | Community-level access, distributed sites, limited service |

---

## Evidence: How Health Systems Describe Themselves

### University of Michigan Health

> *"3 hospitals, 6 specialty treatment centers and over 50 clinics and health centers"*

**Source:** Ann Arbor chamber directory / UM Health self-description

UM Health explicitly distinguishes **three levels** below the system:
1. Hospitals (3)
2. Specialty treatment centers (6)
3. Clinics and health centers (50+)

This is the clearest evidence — a major academic medical center uses exactly the 3-tier language we're proposing.

### Cleveland Clinic

> *"23 hospitals, 276 outpatient facilities"*
> *"18 Family Health and Service Centers across the region"*

**Source:** Cleveland Clinic official website (about.clevelandclinic.org / clevelandclinic.org/locations)

Cleveland Clinic structures facilities as:
1. Main campus + hospitals (23)
2. Outpatient facilities (276) — includes specialty centers, diagnostic centers
3. Family Health and Service Centers (18) — community-access primary care sites

### Kaiser Permanente

> *"40 hospitals and 609 medical facilities"*

**Source:** Kaiser Permanente Fast Facts; Wikipedia

Kaiser's structure:
1. Hospitals (40)
2. Medical facilities — larger outpatient/ambulatory centers
3. Medical office buildings — physician practices, clinics

*Note: Some citations report "616 medical office buildings" — the discrepancy reflects different counting methodologies (facilities vs. buildings). The 3-level structure remains consistent regardless of the count.*

### Mayo Clinic Health System

> *"16 hospitals, 53 multispecialty clinics and one mobile health clinic"*

**Source:** Wikipedia (Mayo Clinic Health System)

Mayo distinguishes:
1. Hospitals (16)
2. Multispecialty clinics (53) — larger, multi-service facilities
3. Provider offices / mobile health — smaller access points

### Beaumont Health / Corewell Health

> *"8 hospitals"* + *"187 ambulatory/outpatient sites including emergency and urgent care, outpatient and medical centers, outpatient laboratory centers, outpatient pharmacies, physical therapy centers, imaging centers, senior living and nursing homes"*

**Source:** Wikipedia (Beaumont Health)

Beaumont's structure:
1. Hospitals (8)
2. Ambulatory/outpatient sites (187) — further subdivided by service type
3. Individual specialty sites (lab centers, imaging centers, etc.)

### Providence Health & Services

> *"51 hospitals and over 1,100 clinics in seven U.S. states"*

**Source:** Wikipedia (Providence Health & Services)

Providence explicitly separates:
1. Hospitals (51)
2. Clinics (1,100+)

*Note: Providence's self-description uses a 2-level structure (hospitals + clinics) without an explicit intermediate tier. This represents a valid alternative model where specialty centers are embedded within hospitals rather than standing as independent branded entities. The 3-tier model still applies where specialty centers have independent brand identity (e.g., Providence Portland Medical Center's specialty programs).*

## Evidence: Academic and Industry Frameworks

### Hub-and-Spoke Model

The hub-and-spoke model is the dominant organizational framework in healthcare systems literature, cited across PMC, HealthLeaders, and industry publications.

> *"The hub is the hospital, and the spokes are a network of strategically placed outpatient clinics within the community."*
> — Hall Render, "Weekly Hospital Real Estate Briefing"

> *"Hub-and-spoke healthcare delivery networks... main campus or hub, which receives the heaviest resource investments and supplies the most intensive medical services, complemented by satellite campuses or spokes, which offer more limited service arrays at sites distributed..."*
> — PMC (Hub-and-Spoke Organization Design)

The model consistently describes three levels:
1. **Hub** = anchor hospital (inpatient, full service)
2. **Intermediate** = specialty centers / ambulatory care centers (focused service)
3. **Spoke** = community clinics / satellite sites (distributed access)

*Note: Academic literature often expresses hub-and-spoke as a 2-level model (hub → spoke). The intermediate tier (specialty centers) is inferred from systems that explicitly distinguish branded specialty centers from community clinics. Where specialty centers lack independent brand identity, they collapse into the spoke level.*

### MedPAC (Medicare Payment Advisory Commission)

> *"...costly site (e.g., ambulatory care center versus a hospital...)"*

**Source:** MedPAC June 2004 Report

MedPAC explicitly distinguishes ambulatory care centers from hospitals as separate facility types with different cost structures — supporting the Tier 1 vs Tier 2 distinction.

### "Medicine Moves to the Mall" (Johns Hopkins University Press)

> *"At the hospital, ambulatory care patients mingle with inpatients... At a satellite center, where well-baby visits and treatment of minor illnesses are the most common services..."*

**Source:** "Medicine Moves to the Mall" by Sloane & Sloane (Johns Hopkins University Press, 2003)

Distinguishes between:
1. Hospital-based ambulatory care (inside Tier 1)
2. Satellite centers (standalone, limited service)

*Note: This source describes a 2-level structure (hospital vs. satellite). The Tier 2 specialty center is not explicitly addressed — it represents the case where a satellite has grown into a branded, multi-service entity with independent identity.*

### PolicyMap Compendium of U.S. Health Systems

> *"The Compendium contains four primary facility files—hospitals, outpatient sites, nursing homes, and home health care organizations"*

**Source:** PolicyMap Blog (December 2025)

A health systems data vendor distinguishes hospitals from outpatient sites as separate facility categories.

## Key Distinction: Tier 2 vs Tier 3

The dividing line between Tier 2 and Tier 3 is **independent brand identity and service array**:

| Criteria | Tier 2 (Specialty Center) | Tier 3 (Clinic) |
|---|---|---|
| Brand identity | Named, branded entity (e.g., "Kellogg Eye Center") | Generic or service-named (e.g., "Urology Clinic") |
| Service array | Multi-service within specialty | Single-service or limited |
| Physical footprint | Dedicated building or major wing | Room, suite, or small building |
| Patient volume | High-volume specialty destination | Community access point |
| Example | Karmanos Cancer Institute | Pediatric Cardiology in Flint |

## International Evidence

### SingHealth (Singapore)

SingHealth uses a hub-and-spoke model with:
1. Acute hospitals (hub)
2. Specialist centers
3. Polyclinics / community health centers (spoke)

**Source:** Straits Times; SingHealth website

*Note: International evidence is limited. SingHealth provides one data point supporting the 3-level structure, but cross-cultural healthcare system comparisons require caution. The model is primarily validated against US health systems.*

## Counter-Evidence: Systems That Don't Fit

Not all health systems follow the 3-tier model. Several represent valid alternatives:

### Flat Structures (2 Levels)

Some systems describe themselves with only two levels below the root:

- **Some community health systems:** Hospital + clinics, no independent specialty centers
- **Providence Health & Services:** "51 hospitals and over 1,100 clinics" — specialty programs embedded within hospitals rather than standing as independent entities
- **Some rural health networks:** Hospital + satellite clinics, no intermediate tier

### Hybrid Structures

Other systems use the 3-tier model but with different naming or grouping:

- **Mayo Clinic:** "16 hospitals, 53 multispecialty clinics" — the "multispecialty clinic" label could map to either Tier 2 (if branded/independent) or Tier 3 (if service-named)
- **Beaumont/Corewell:** "8 hospitals, 187 ambulatory/outpatient sites" — the 187 sites are further subdivided by service type, suggesting an internal hierarchy within Tier 2/3

### Implications

The 3-tier model is **descriptive, not prescriptive**. It captures what major academic medical centers and large integrated systems actually look like, but:

1. **Specialty centers without independent brand identity** collapse into the clinic tier
2. **Systems without significant specialty programs** may only need 2 tiers
3. **The model is most useful for academic health systems** and large integrated networks

The Tier 2 vs Tier 3 distinction (independent brand identity + service array) remains the key decision rule for classification.

---

## Conclusion

The evidence is consistent across:
- **Self-descriptions** of major US health systems (UM, Cleveland Clinic, Kaiser, Mayo, Beaumont, Providence)
- **Academic literature** (hub-and-spoke model, PMC papers)
- **Industry frameworks** (MedPAC, PolicyMap)
- **International systems** (SingHealth)

All point to a **3-level structure below the health system root**: hospitals → specialty centers → clinics/satellites.

**Recommendation: Move clinics to Tier 3.**
